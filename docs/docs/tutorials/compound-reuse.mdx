# Compound Reuse

If you built the subsumption architecture from the previous tutorial, you probably noticed that the diagram got complex fast — dozens of nodes and connections sprawling across the canvas. The escape latch alone takes four nodes. Add avoidance, cruise, and the gating logic, and it becomes hard to see the overall structure.

Compounds solve this. They let you take a cluster of nodes — like the entire escape behavior — and collapse it into a single box with named inputs and outputs. The internal wiring still exists, but it's tucked away behind a clean interface. You can also reuse compounds: build a "noise-filtered sensor" once and stamp out copies for every sensor on the robot.

On the surface this tutorial is about tidiness — collapsing a sprawl of nodes into a clean box. But there's a bigger idea underneath. Once you can package "calibrated sensor" or "escape behavior" as a reusable unit, you stop thinking in wires and start thinking in *concepts*. That move — from raw mechanism to named, reusable parts — is arguably how any complicated mind gets built at all, including the one reading this.

**Time:** 15 minutes
**Prerequisites:** Completed at least [Obstacle Avoidance](./obstacle-avoidance)

## Scenario: different sensors, same calibration

The robot has four inputs it steers by: two **ToF distance sensors** on the front (XSHUT pins `8` and `12`) for obstacles, and two **photocells** (`A0` and `A1`) for light. Four sensors — but only two *kinds* of sensor.

Despite being different hardware, all four want the same preprocessing before their signal is trustworthy: a dead-zone transfer curve to throw away faint, jittery low readings below 15, followed by a threshold at 30 that turns the cleaned-up signal into a crisp "detected / not detected." Without compounds you'd wire that two-step circuit four separate times — and if you later decided the dead zone should be 20 instead of 15, you'd have to find and fix it in four places and hope you didn't miss one.

The insight that makes this powerful: **the calibration doesn't care what kind of sensor feeds it.** Every source in BraitenBot — ToF, photocell, color channel — emits the same 0–100 signal. So a single "calibrated sensor" compound can sit downstream of *any* of them. Build it once; reuse it across the whole robot, regardless of the hardware upstream.

With compounds, you build the preprocessing once, group it, and drag out four instances — one for each physical sensor.

## Step 1: Build the calibration circuit

Because the compound should work for *any* sensor type, we keep the physical sensor **outside** the compound and put only the signal processing inside. Grouping needs at least two nodes, so we use a Summation as a simple pass-through gateway for the incoming signal:

1. Add a **Summation** node (label "In", weight 1.0 — it just passes its input through)
2. Add a **Threshold** node (threshold 30, label "Detected")
3. Connect In → Detected (weight 1.0)
4. On this connection, set the **Transfer Function** to **Non-linear (curve)** and draw a dead-zone curve: points at (-100, 0), (15, 0), (100, 100)

Test in trace mode: slide the input from 0 to 50. The threshold outputs 0 for readings below 30 and 100 above — and the dead zone also ensures that noisy low readings (0–15) are zeroed out before they even reach the threshold.

This is the kind of preprocessing you'd want on every sensor. Without it, electrical noise at low sensor readings (5–15) can cause spurious threshold triggers, making the robot twitch randomly.

## Step 2: Group into a compound

1. **Shift+click** both the Summation and Threshold nodes to select them
2. Click **Group** in the toolbar

BraitenBot creates a compound type and replaces the two nodes with a single compound instance. Because the Summation's input came from outside the selection, it becomes an **input port** on the compound; because the Threshold's output went outside, it becomes an **output port**.

Rename the compound: click it, and in the config panel change the label to **"Calibrated Sensor."**

## Step 3: Inspect the body

**Double-click** the compound instance to enter the body editor. Inside you'll see:

- A **Compound Input** port node, feeding the Summation
- The original Summation and Threshold nodes
- A **Compound Output** port node, connected to the threshold's output
- The dead-zone transfer curve is preserved on the internal connection

This is the compound's "schematic." Any changes you make here — adjusting the threshold, modifying the transfer curve — will affect **every instance** of this compound type.

Click the breadcrumb to return to the main canvas.

## Step 4: Stamp out more instances

In the **Node Palette**, a new **Compounds** section has appeared with your "Calibrated Sensor" type. Drag out three more instances — you now have four identical calibrators.

Notice what you *don't* have to configure: there's no pin to set on a Calibrated Sensor, because the pins live on the external sensor nodes, not inside the compound. That's exactly why keeping the sensor outside was the right call.

:::note[Why the sensor stays outside]

It's tempting to drop the ToF sensor *inside* the compound so "Calibrated Sensor" is a complete, self-contained unit. Don't. Every instance of a compound shares one body, so all four would share a single XSHUT pin — and worse, they'd all have to be ToF sensors, making the compound useless for the photocells. Keeping the sensor outside is what lets the *same* calibration serve four sensors across two different hardware types.

:::

## Step 5: Wire each sensor into its own instance

Connect each physical sensor's output to a different compound instance's input port:

- Left Obstacle (ToF, XSHUT 8) → Calibrated Sensor #1
- Right Obstacle (ToF, XSHUT 12) → Calibrated Sensor #2
- Left Light (photocell, A0) → Calibrated Sensor #3
- Right Light (photocell, A1) → Calibrated Sensor #4

All four now apply identical internal calibration to whatever their sensor sends. Change the threshold in one compound body and all four update at once — exactly the consistency you want when tuning a robot. Two of the instances are cleaning up laser-distance data and two are cleaning up photocell data, but the compound neither knows nor cares. That indifference is the whole point of an abstraction.

## Step 6: Wire compound outputs into behavior

The cleaned-up detections wire into your main diagram like any other connection. You can even drive two different behaviors from the four instances:

- **Avoidance** — Left Obstacle's calibrated output → **Right** Motor summation (weight -1.0), Right Obstacle's → **Left** Motor summation (weight -1.0). Cross-wired inhibition, exactly as in [Obstacle Avoidance](./obstacle-avoidance).
- **Light-seeking** — Left Light's calibrated output → **Right** Motor summation (weight +0.7), Right Light's → **Left** Motor summation (weight +0.7). Cross-wired excitation, as in [Light Follower](./light-follower).

The main canvas shows four clean boxes feeding the motor summations, instead of a dozen nodes of duplicated preprocessing — and the same calibrator does double duty across two behaviors built from two sensor types.

## Packaging behavior layers as compounds

Compounds aren't just for sensor preprocessing. In the [subsumption tutorial](./subsumption-architecture), the escape behavior consists of a bumper sensor, a latch (summation + delay + feedback), a threshold, and a reverse constant. That's five nodes implementing a single concept: "reverse for 2 seconds when bumped."

Select all five nodes and group them into an "Escape Behavior" compound with:

- **Input port**: Bumper signal
- **Output ports**: Motor Inhibition (the -100 signal), Is Active (the threshold output)

Now the top level of your subsumption diagram shows three clean boxes — Escape, Avoid, Cruise — wired into the final motor summations. The intent of the architecture is immediately visible, and the implementation details are one double-click away.

## Multi-port compounds

Compounds can have any number of input and output ports. The avoidance behavior, for example, might have:

- **Inputs**: Left Proximity, Right Proximity
- **Outputs**: Left Motor Command, Right Motor Command, Is Active

When you group nodes, BraitenBot automatically creates port anchors for every connection that crosses the selection boundary. You can also add port anchors manually inside the body by dragging **Compound Input** or **Compound Output** from the palette (these appear in the palette only while editing a compound body).

Name the port nodes descriptively — "Left Proximity" rather than "Compound Input 1." These names appear as labels on the instance's connection handles, making it clear what each port expects.

## Ungrouping

Sometimes you need to modify one instance differently from the others — for example, the photocell instances need a lower threshold than the ToF instances, because light readings and distance readings occupy different parts of the 0–100 range. Select the compound instance, click **Ungroup**, and the body nodes are inlined back into the main diagram. The compound type still exists for other instances; ungrouping only affects the one you selected.

## Design tips

**Keep compounds focused.** "Calibrated Sensor" and "Escape Behavior" are good compound names because they describe a single concept. "Left Side Processing" is too broad — if you need to change the avoidance threshold independently from the sensor calibration, a single compound for both will get in the way.

**One level of nesting is usually enough.** You can put compound instances inside compound bodies, and BraitenBot handles this correctly. But debugging a problem three levels deep is frustrating. For most robots, sensor preprocessing compounds + behavior compounds is sufficient.

**Wheel motors stay outside.** The left and right wheel motors are always on the main canvas, attached to the robot overlay. They can't be inside compounds. All your compound outputs eventually wire into the motor summation nodes at the top level.

:::note[Minds are built from reused parts]

A compound is an abstraction: a chunk of circuit you can name, trust, and then stop thinking about. This is the only known way to build anything truly complex — hide the details of a working sub-part behind a clean interface, then use it as a single move in a larger design. Software does it with functions; brains appear to do it with reusable circuit motifs; evolution does it shamelessly, copying a working sub-system and repurposing it rather than inventing from scratch.

Notice that the abstraction is purely *for you*. The generated robot code doesn't know your compounds exist — at upload time they're flattened back into the same flat mesh of nodes and wires the robot actually runs. The hierarchy is a tool for the designer's mind, not the machine's. Which is a clue worth keeping: the modules we "see" in a complex system are often a story we tell to make it thinkable, as much as a fact about how it runs.

:::

## What you've learned

Compounds are how you scale from simple Braitenberg vehicles to complex, multi-behavior robots without the diagram becoming unmanageable. They don't change the underlying circuit — they're purely an organizational tool — but they let you think at the right level of abstraction: individual sensor calibration, complete behavior modules, or the overall architecture. And because a well-drawn compound depends only on the *signal* it receives, not the hardware behind it, one definition can serve every sensor on the robot at once.

### Questions to sit with

- Your compounds vanish at upload — the robot runs as one flat mesh. If the modular structure isn't in the machine, is it "really there," or only in your understanding of the machine?
- One "Calibrated Sensor" served a laser rangefinder and a photocell without distinguishing them. When you reuse a concept across very different situations, are you seeing a real shared structure, or just declining to look closer?
- Reuse let you build something complex without holding all of it in your head at once. Is that a limitation you worked around, or is *abstraction itself* what makes complex behavior possible in the first place?
