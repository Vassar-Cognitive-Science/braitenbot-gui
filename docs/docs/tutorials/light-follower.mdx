# Fear & Love

Your first vehicle had one eye, so it could only ever speed up or slow down — never steer. Give it a second eye and a choice appears that didn't exist before: which side's wire runs to which wheel. That single choice is the whole difference between Braitenberg's **Vehicle 2b**, which chases light, and **Vehicle 2a**, which flees it.

This is the moment Braitenberg's vehicles start to feel uncanny. The same two sensors and two motors, wired one way, produce a creature that *chases* light; wired the other way, one that *flees* it. Nothing about the hardware changes — only which wire crosses to which wheel. As you play, notice how strongly the behavior invites an emotional reading, and how little is actually there to do any feeling.

## Why cross-wiring creates steering

A two-wheeled robot steers by running its wheels at different speeds: if the right wheel spins faster than the left, the robot turns left, like a tank. Cross-wiring exploits this. When the light is to the left, the left sensor reads high and drives the **right** motor faster — turning the robot **left**, toward the light. The stronger the difference between the two sides, the sharper the turn; when the light is centered, both motors match and the vehicle drives straight.

Now wire it yourself.

## The fearful twin

Swap the cross-wiring for same-side wiring — left eye to left wheel, right eye to right wheel — and the identical hardware does the opposite: it turns **away** from light. Strong light on the left speeds up the *left* wheel, pushing the robot's nose to the right, into the dark. This is Vehicle 2a.

Compare the diagram below with the cross-wired one above — the nodes and weights are identical, only the wire destinations have swapped. Drag the left sensor up and watch the **left** motor accelerate this time.

## Puzzle 2 — Rewire love into fear

Reading that a single crossed wire separates love from fear is one thing; feeling it under your fingers is another. The diagram below starts as the cross-wired **chaser** you just finished. To make the switch:

1. **Delete both crossed wires.** Click a connection's weight badge and use **Delete connection** in the popover.

Now drag the Left Eye's slider up. In the chaser, the *right* wheel raced ahead; in your rewired version, the *left* wheel should accelerate, pushing the vehicle's nose *away* from the light. Nothing about the eyes or wheels changed — only which wire meets which wheel.

Notice what you *didn't* have to do: there was no rule to write, no "if light then flee" to encode. Fear here is a fact about wiring and a world with a light in it — exactly Braitenberg's point, now sitting in your own edited diagram.

:::note[Thinking like Braitenberg]

You now have two vehicles, identical down to the last component, that an observer would describe in opposite emotional terms: one *loves* the light and pursues it, the other *fears* it and flees. Braitenberg's unsettling claim is that the emotion lives entirely in the eye of the observer — the vehicles differ by a single crossed wire, not by a feeling.

He took the moth further than a metaphor. A moth spiraling into a flame isn't suicidal and isn't in love; it is (roughly) a cross-wired vehicle whose ancient navigation trick — hold a fixed angle to a faraway light — collapses when the light is suddenly close. The "tragedy" is ours to narrate. The mechanism is just wiring meeting a world it wasn't built for.
:::

## Puzzle 3 — Keep it moving in the dark

Both of your vehicles share an embarrassing flaw: in total darkness they stop dead. No light, no signal, no motion — a chaser that starves the moment the room goes dark.

The cure is a **Constant** node: a steady value that never changes, feeding both wheels through **Summation** nodes so the sensor signals can add steering *on top of* a baseline forward drive. The diagram below has the whole cross-wired chaser rebuilt through summations, plus a Forward constant of 30 sitting there, connected to nothing. Wire **Forward** into both summations (the default weight of 1.0 is exactly right).

Once both wires are in, the vehicle always drives forward at 30 — and when light appears to one side, the sensor signal *adds* to the baseline on the opposite motor, steering on top of the forward motion. Behavior stacked on behavior, with nothing but addition.

## Building it in the app

Everything on this page works the same way in the desktop app: the two photocell nodes live in the **Basic** tab's Kit sensors group (pre-wired to A0/A1), Summation is in the Compute group, and Constant is on the **Advanced** tab. Wire, set weights, and use **Trace Signal Flow** — the [editor guide](../getting-started/editor) walks the interface.

## What you've learned

By changing only the wiring pattern — same-side vs. cross-wired — the same hardware produces opposite behaviors: attraction or avoidance. No programming logic changed; the behavior emerges entirely from which wire goes where. And "fear" and "love" turned out to be words *you* supplied while watching. The vehicles hold only wires; the emotions were standing on your side of the glass the whole time.

### Questions to sit with

- A single sign flip turns "fear" into "love." If an emotion in a vehicle is that cheap, how confidently should we read emotions into *anything* whose wiring we can't see?
- The cross-wired robot only "seeks" light because of the world it's in; in a uniformly lit room the behavior vanishes. How much of what looks like a goal is really a property of the environment?
- Would you be comfortable saying the moth "wants" to reach the flame? If not, where is the line between the moth and the robot — and is there one?
