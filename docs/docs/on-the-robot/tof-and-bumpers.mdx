# Rangefinders & Bumpers

You built the avoider in [3. Don't](../lessons/obstacle-avoidance) and gave it a memory in [5. Memory](../lessons/latches-with-delay) — now mount the sensors that make both circuits real.

## Mounting the ToF sensors

Mount the robot's two **ToF distance sensors** (VL53L4CD) on the front, angled outward about 30° from center — one pointing front-left, one front-right. Each one measures the actual distance to whatever is in front of it with a tiny time-of-flight laser and reports it over the shared I2C bus. The **ToF Distance** node scales that reading to 0–100, with a *closer* object reading *higher* and an object at the maximum distance you set reading 0.

### XSHUT wiring

**Left ToF Distance** uses XSHUT on **D8**, **Right ToF Distance** on **D12** — both must be wired, or one sensor won't come up. See [Supported Hardware ▸ I2C pins](../hardware/supported-hardware#i2c-pins) for why each ToF sensor needs its own XSHUT pin.

## Calibrating the threshold

Because the ToF reports distance, the threshold is easy to reason about: with the **ToF Distance** node's max distance left at its default of 500 mm, a closer object reads higher, so a threshold of 60 fires at roughly 200 mm (about 20 cm).

To find the right threshold for your build, put the robot near a wall at the distance you want it to start turning and read the value off — see [Calibrating with trace mode](../guide/simulation#calibrating-with-trace-mode) for the ritual — then set the threshold just below it.

The right threshold depends on your specific sensors and how they're mounted:

- **Too low** (e.g., 20): the robot reacts to distant objects and never gets close to anything — overly cautious, may get stuck oscillating
- **Too high** (e.g., 90): the robot doesn't react until it's almost touching the obstacle — may clip corners
- **About right** (e.g., 50–70): the robot turns about 15–20 cm from obstacles

## Tuning the inhibition weight

The weight on the inhibitory connections controls how sharply the robot turns:

| Weight | Effect |
|--------|--------|
| -0.5 | The opposite motor slows to 0 but doesn't reverse — gentle, wide turns. Good for open spaces. |
| -1.0 | The opposite motor reverses to -50 — sharp pivot turns. Good for tight spaces like corridors. |

Weights are limited to the range -1 to +1, so **-1.0 is the sharpest single-connection inhibition available**. If you need an even harder turn, lower the **Constant** node's value (so there's less forward drive to overcome) rather than reaching for a weight below -1.

## The bumpers

The ToF sensors miss things — a thin chair leg, or a surface angled so the laser beam scatters away instead of bouncing back. The bump switches are the backstop.

On the physical robot, the front-left bump switch is wired between pin **D2** and ground, and the front-right between **D3** and ground. The **Bump Front-Left** and **Bump Front-Right** presets come with **INPUT_PULLUP** enabled — so pressing the switch reads as 100 in BraitenBot. See [Digital sensors](../hardware/supported-hardware#digital-sensors) for why pullup mode inverts the reading like that.

:::note[A real bump is over before you see it]
On real switches the contact is momentary — maybe 50 ms, only a few loop iterations. By the time the next loop runs, the bumper is already open again and the event is gone. That's why the bumper feeds a latch rather than a motor directly: the circuit from [5. Memory](../lessons/latches-with-delay) captures the click and holds it long enough to act on.
:::
