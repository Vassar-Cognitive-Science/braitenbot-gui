# The Color Eye

You taught a vehicle taste in [4. Taste](../lessons/color-discrimination). Now bolt the eye to the robot's belly and calibrate it for real floors.

## Mounting

Mount the **TCS34725 color sensor** on the underside of the robot, facing straight down at the floor, about a centimeter above the surface. The module has a bright white LED that floods the patch directly beneath it, so the reading depends mostly on what color is under the sensor rather than on room lighting.

Like the ToF sensors, it lives on the shared I2C bus. There's no pin to assign, since its address (`0x29`) is fixed in hardware.

## The test course

Lay out some colored paper patches on the floor: a red one, a blue one, and (to keep the discrimination circuit honest) a plain white one. The robot will cruise forward and react as it rolls over each. If the circuit is right, it lunges over red, recoils from blue, and rolls straight across the white paper without a flicker.

## Calibrating for real color

The channel numbers in the lesson are illustrative: your surfaces, lighting, and mounting height will shift them. A few things to get right on real hardware:

- **Sensor height.** The white LED's usefulness depends on being close to the surface. Too high and it reads a dim blur averaged with room light; too low and it can scrape the floor. Aim for roughly a centimeter, and keep it consistent.
- **Gain.** The color node has a **gain** setting (1×, 4×, 16×, or 60×; default 16×). If every channel reads near 0 on your patches, raise the gain to amplify dim reflections. If white paper pins channels at a flat 100 (saturated, so red and blue can't be told apart), lower the gain until they come off the ceiling. You need headroom on *both* channels for the subtraction to mean anything.
- **Reaction time.** The sensor produces a fresh reading about ten times a second. A fast robot can shoot clean across a small patch between readings, so keep the cruise speed modest or make the patches generous.
- **Ambient light.** The onboard LED dominates at close range, but a beam of direct sunlight or a colored desk lamp can still bias the channels. If the robot behaves differently at different times of day, that's your culprit: shade the sensor or recalibrate the thresholds for the room it runs in.

:::tip[Read the real numbers first]
Park the robot over each patch and read the actual channel readings. See [Calibrating with trace mode](../guide/simulation#calibrating-with-trace-mode). Set your opponent-signal thresholds against *those* numbers, not the ones in the lesson.
:::
