# Nobody Home

So far every creature you've built has had one behavior: drive toward light, veer from walls, reverse on a bump. A real robot needs all of them at once — and the moment you say that, a question sneaks in. When cruise says "forward" and avoidance says "hard right" at the same instant, *who decides?*

This lesson quietly abandons an assumption you might not know you were making: that somewhere in a behaving robot there is a *decider* — a central place where options are weighed and a choice is issued. There isn't one here. Cruise, avoid, and escape all run at once, blind to each other, and "what the robot does" is simply whatever their signals sum to. As you wire it up, look for the spot where you expect a controller to live — and notice the robot works fine without it.

You've already built every part. The cruise-plus-inhibition pattern is [3. Don't](./obstacle-avoidance); the decaying bump latch is [5. Memory](./latches-with-delay). Today you stack them into three layers, with one rule doing all the arbitration: **higher-priority behaviors suppress lower-priority ones by outvoting them at a summation.**

Three clusters, all feeding the same two Mix summations. **Cruise** (the constant, middle) always says +50 forward. **Avoid** (the ToF distance sensors and thresholds, top) says −100 to one wheel when a wall gets close. **Escape** (the bumper and decay latch, left) says −100 to *both* wheels for about a second after a bump. Every layer runs all the time; none of them checks whether another is active. Priority lives in the wiring — an inhibitory −100 always outvotes cruise's +50 at a summation — and the summation is the whole arbitration mechanism. When the strong signals go quiet, cruise is simply what's left.

Now build the pecking order yourself, one layer at a time.

### How suppression works here

Walk the arithmetic for a wall on the left: Left Distance reads 80, Left Detect fires at 100, and the −1.0 weight delivers −100 to the Right Mix.

| Input to Right Mix | Value |
|---|---|
| Cruise | +50 |
| Left Detect (cross-wired) | −100 |
| **Total** | **−50** |

The left wheel stays at 50, the right wheel reverses, and the robot pivots away. This *is* suppression: cruise is still running — still faithfully contributing its +50 — but avoidance's −100 overwhelms it and takes effective control of that motor. The cruise layer is suppressed, not turned off. The moment the wall clears, the −100 disappears and cruise's +50 is revealed again, seamlessly, with no transition logic anywhere.

## Puzzle 2 — Install the veto

The two-layer system handles most rooms, but rangefinders have blind spots: a thin chair leg, a surface angled so the laser scatters away instead of bouncing back. Sometimes the first the robot knows of an obstacle is the *thud*. That's what the bumper is for — and the bump response must outrank everything, both wheels, no exceptions.

The escape cluster is pre-built on the left; it's the decay latch from [5. Memory](./latches-with-delay). A bump charges the Escape Latch, the 20 ms Delay feeds 95% of it back each tick, and the sum decays geometrically — staying above Escape Active's threshold of 10 for about 45 ticks, roughly a second, before snapping back to 0. (The embed's weight slider moves in steps of 0.05; in the app you can set finer feedback weights, like 0.98 for a hold of about 2.3 seconds.)

Right now the latch is shouting into the void: Escape Active connects to nothing. Give it the veto — wire it into **both** Mix nodes at **−1.0**.

Notice what makes escape the top layer. It isn't a bigger number — its wires are −1.0, exactly like avoidance's. Unlike avoidance, which inhibits only the *opposite* wheel (producing a turn), escape inhibits *both* wheels symmetrically (producing a straight reverse), and the latch sustains it after the bump ends. Escape's priority comes from its topology and timing, not from an out-of-range weight.

:::note[Why not -1.5?]

It's tempting to give escape a weight like -1.5 to "overpower" both lower layers at once, but that isn't possible — weights clamp to the range **-1 to +1**. A single connection can scale a 0–100 signal to at most ±100. To suppress a lower layer more aggressively than that, gate it (for example with a Multiply node) or restructure the layering — don't reach for a weight outside ±1.
:::

### When layers collide

Two more scenarios, and the arithmetic settles both without a referee.

**Bumper hit *while* avoiding a wall.** Bumper pressed and Left Distance at 80 simultaneously:

| Input to Right Mix | Value |
|---|---|
| Cruise | +50 |
| Left Detect (avoid) | −100 |
| Escape Active | −100 |
| **Total** | **−150 → clamped to −100** |

| Input to Left Mix | Value |
|---|---|
| Cruise | +50 |
| Escape Active | −100 |
| **Total** | **−50** |

Right motor at −100, left motor at −50 — both wheels reverse, the right one harder because avoidance reinforces escape on that side. The robot backs away from the wall, curving as it goes. The two inhibitory layers stack rather than fight.

**Escape ends, obstacle still present.** About a second after the bump the latch decays below 10 and Escape Active drops to 0. Left Distance still reads 80:

| Input to Right Mix | Value |
|---|---|
| Cruise | +50 |
| Left Detect (avoid) | −100 |
| Escape Active | 0 |
| **Total** | **−50** |

The robot immediately resumes steering right — avoidance picks up seamlessly where it left off. No gap, no handoff protocol. The layers just produce their signals independently, and the summation sorts out which behavior dominates.

That is subsumption's central virtue: **behaviors are independent modules that don't know about each other.** Avoid never checks whether escape is active. Escape can't switch avoidance off. They emit signals, and the math resolves the argument at the wheels. You can add or remove layers without touching the existing ones.

:::note[Intelligence without a boss]

No part of this robot is "in charge." No layer knows the others exist; none can switch another off by command. Priority isn't a decision anyone makes — it falls out of topology and arithmetic, the way -100 simply outweighs +50 at a summation. The competent-looking whole has no center.

This is the radical idea Rodney Brooks built behavior-based robotics on in the 1980s: **intelligence without representation**. Skip the central model of the world and the planner that reasons over it; layer many simple, tightly-coupled sensor-to-motor behaviors and let conflicts resolve at the wheels, not in a mind. It works unnervingly well — and it raises a question that won't quite go away: if a robot can be this competent with nobody home, how sure are you that *you* have a boss inside, rather than a very good committee?
:::

:::tip[Go further: more layers]

The pattern scales. A **wander layer** — an Oscillator feeding both Mix nodes at ±0.3 — makes the robot gently weave while cruising, and avoidance overrides it for free because −100 outvotes ±30. A **light-seek layer** between avoid and escape is just the cross-wired photocells from [2. Fear & Love](./fear-and-love) at ±0.8. For each new layer, pick a weight magnitude stronger than the layers it should suppress and weaker than the layers that should suppress it.
:::

## Puzzle 3 — Your diagram got unwieldy

Look back at what you just wired: thirteen nodes, fourteen connections, and the *idea* — three layers in a pecking order — is nowhere on the canvas. You have to squint to see the architecture through the spaghetti. But you already know the clusters have names. Four nodes on the left are "escape." Four up top are "avoid." Name them, and the diagram becomes three boxes.

That's what a **compound** is: a cluster of nodes collapsed into a single box with named input and output ports. The internal wiring still exists — every instance shares one body — but from the outside it's a single part with a clean interface. Below, Escape and Avoid have been boxed for you. The sensors feed the boxes' input ports, Avoid's two inhibit outputs are already cross-wired into the Mixes, and everything else is exactly the machine you built. One thing is missing: Escape's **Suppress** port connects to nothing.

Wire Suppress into both Mix nodes at **−1.0** — and let the goal tests prove the boxed machine is the same animal.

### Making boxes in the app

In the desktop app you build compounds from any cluster you've wired: **Shift+click** to select the nodes (say, the latch, delay, and threshold of the escape cluster), then click **Group** in the toolbar. BraitenBot collapses the selection into a single compound instance, automatically creating an **input port** for every connection that entered the selection from outside and an **output port** for every connection that left it — name the ports descriptively, since those names label the instance's connection handles. **Double-click** an instance to enter the body editor and adjust the internals; every instance of the type shares one body, so a change inside propagates to all of them. Once a type exists, a **Compounds** section appears in the palette and you can stamp out as many instances as you like. One design tip covers most mistakes: **wheels and sensors stay outside the box** — motors live on the robot overlay, and hardware pins belong on individual sensor nodes, not in a shared body.

That last clause hints at the real payoff. Tidiness is the smaller half of compounds; the bigger half is **reuse**. Because every source in BraitenBot emits the same 0–100 signal, a compound that processes signals doesn't care what hardware feeds it. :::note[Why the sensor stays outside]

It's tempting to drop the ToF sensor *inside* the compound so "Calibrated Sensor" is a complete, self-contained unit. Don't. Every instance of a compound shares one body, so all four would share a single XSHUT pin — and worse, they'd all have to be ToF sensors, making the compound useless for the photocells. Keeping the sensor outside is what lets the *same* calibration serve four sensors across two different hardware types.

:::

:::note[Minds are built from reused parts]

A compound is an abstraction: a chunk of circuit you can name, trust, and then stop thinking about. This is the only known way to build anything truly complex — hide the details of a working sub-part behind a clean interface, then use it as a single move in a larger design. Software does it with functions; brains appear to do it with reusable circuit motifs; evolution does it shamelessly, copying a working sub-system and repurposing it rather than inventing from scratch.

Notice that the abstraction is purely *for you*. The generated robot code doesn't know your compounds exist — at upload time they're flattened back into the same flat mesh of nodes and wires the robot actually runs. The hierarchy is a tool for the designer's mind, not the machine's. Which is a clue worth keeping: the modules we "see" in a complex system are often a story we tell to make it thinkable, as much as a fact about how it runs.

:::

## What you've learned

You stacked three independent behaviors — cruise, avoid, escape — into a robot that navigates, dodges, and recovers, with no controller anywhere in it. Priority is nothing but topology and arithmetic: an inhibitory −100 outvotes a +50 at a summation, escape outranks avoid by inhibiting both wheels and latching, and when the loud layers fall silent, cruise is simply what remains. Then you named the clusters, and the same machine became three legible boxes — an abstraction that changed how you see the robot without changing the robot at all.

### Questions to sit with

- Nothing in this robot chooses between behaviors, yet it reliably does the "right" one. Does arbitration require an arbiter — or just the right place for signals to collide?
- Watching the finished robot dodge, reverse, and resume, you'll instinctively narrate one continuous agent. But it's three deaf modules summing into a wheel. Which is the illusion — the unified agent, or the separateness of the parts?
- Your compounds vanish at upload — the robot runs as one flat mesh. If the modular structure isn't in the machine, is it "really there," or only in your understanding of the machine?
- Reuse let you build something complex without holding all of it in your head at once. Is that a limitation you worked around, or is *abstraction itself* what makes complex behavior possible in the first place?
