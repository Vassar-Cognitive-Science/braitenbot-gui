# Your First Vehicle

Imagine a moth drawn to a porch light: it simply moves faster when light hits its sensors. That's the idea behind a Braitenberg vehicle: wire a sensor directly to a motor and behavior emerges from the connection itself.

This is Braitenberg's **Vehicle 1**, the first machine in his 1984 book *Vehicles: Experiments in Synthetic Psychology*. His method was to build minds: start with a trivial mechanism, set it loose, and watch how quickly we reach for words like "wants," "likes," and "afraid" to describe it. (For the full tour of vehicles, see [Braitenberg Vehicles](../guide/braitenberg-vehicles).)

Every circuit on this page is live. Start by playing.

The brighter the light, the faster both wheels turn, and because they always turn at exactly the same speed, this creature can only ever drive straight. There is no program in it; the wiring produces the behavior directly.

Now build it yourself, one move at a time.

That wire you just drew is the whole idea. A connection carries the sensor's value to the motor, and the connection's **weight** decides how much of it arrives. Both wires here have weight 1.0: a light reading of 75 becomes a motor speed of 75, unmodified.

## Puzzle 3: Now make light repel it

Weights can be negative. A negative weight turns the wire into a "don't": the brighter the light, the harder the motor drives *backward*.

Stop and notice what just happened. You didn't add a "flee" module or write an avoidance rule. You changed the *sign of a number*, and an observer watching the physical robot would tell you it's a different animal, one that backs away from flashlights instead of chasing them.

:::note[Thinking like Braitenberg]

Watch someone meet this robot for the first time. They'll say it "likes" the light, or "is looking for" the flashlight, the language of goals and desires. But you built it, so you know there is no goal anywhere inside it: only a wire that turns brightness into speed.

Braitenberg called this the **law of uphill analysis and downhill invention**: it is far easier to *create* a behavior than to *deduce* the mechanism behind one. An observer reasoning backward from what the robot does would invent something far more elaborate than the truth. We do exactly this every day when we explain other people, and perhaps even ourselves. Sitting with that possibility is the real point of the exercise; the robot is just the excuse.
:::

## What you've learned

With one sensor and two wires you built a vehicle that responds to its environment, then rebuilt its whole personality twice without adding a single part. There's no program logic anywhere. The behavior is entirely a product of the wiring: which nodes connect, and with what weight.

### Questions to sit with

- If a friend insisted the robot "wants" the light, what is the simplest experiment that would change their mind? Would it change *yours*?
- The behavior isn't really in the robot or in the room, but in their interaction. Where, exactly, is "moving toward light" located: in the sensor, the wire, the motor, or the flashlight?
- You wrote no rules, yet the robot reliably does something that looks purposeful. How much of a *living* animal's behavior, do you think, might also run without rules?

This vehicle has a limitation: with one sensor it can only speed up or slow down, never steer. Giving it a second eye, and choosing how the two eyes cross to the two wheels, is where things get genuinely strange.
