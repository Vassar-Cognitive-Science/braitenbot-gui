# Memory

Every vehicle you've built so far is **purely reactive**: its output depends only on what the sensors read *right now*. A vehicle like that lives in an eternal present, with no "a moment ago." Its sensors make this worse, not better: they report the present *continuously*, so a brief bump against a wall is, on the very next reading, simply gone. Nothing in the circuit ever saw a "then."

That matters the moment you want a behavior to outlast its cause. A bumper tap lasts a twentieth of a second; "back away from the wall" needs to last a second or two. So where could a memory possibly live? Not in any single part, since none of them store anything. The answer is **topology**: route a signal back into itself through a delay, and the loop keeps that signal alive after its cause has vanished. This lesson builds "the past" out of components that only ever sense "now."

This is a **latch**: a Summation node sends its output through a Delay node (set to 20 ms, exactly one loop tick), which feeds the value back into the Summation one tick later. Walk the loop:

1. **Before the tap**: the Latch receives 0 from the bumper and 0 from the feedback. Output 0. Motor off.
2. **Bumper pressed**: the bumper sends 100. The Latch outputs 100. The delay captures it.
3. **Bumper released**: the bumper sends 0, but the delay hands back the *previous* output. The Latch receives 0 + 100 = 100. It holds.
4. **Every tick after**: the delay keeps echoing, the Latch keeps summing. The tap is remembered indefinitely.

The motor keeps driving even though the bumper was only pressed for a moment. The feedback loop sustains the signal on its own.

:::note[Memory without a memory]

Look closely at what just happened: the latch "remembers" the bump, yet nowhere in the circuit is there a part that *holds* the value 100. The summation only ever adds its inputs in the current instant; the delay only ever echoes what it heard one tick ago. The memory isn't stored anywhere: it lives in the **circulation**, a signal handed from delay to summation and back, around and around.

This is a strange idea, and it isn't just a trick of the editor. Many models of biological short-term memory work the same way: a memory is held not by a dedicated "storage" cell but by a loop of neurons that keep re-exciting each other. Break the loop for a single tick and the memory is gone, instantly and completely, with no trace left behind. On this view, a thought is something a circuit does.
:::

Now build it yourself, one wire at a time.

## Puzzle 2: Teach it to forget

A memory that never fades is a problem: below, the latch drives a Threshold node named "Still stinging?" (it fires 100 whenever the latch is above 10), and the feedback wire is at 1.0. Tap the bumper and watch the Latch sum climb tick after tick and never let go. No input will release it.

The fix is to make the feedback *lossy*. Click the weight badge on the Delay → Latch wire and set it to **0.95**. Now each trip around the loop returns 95% of the value: the tap charges the sum up to a few hundred, and once the bumper releases, the echo decays geometrically (452, 429, 408, …) until it slips under the threshold and the sting goes quiet.

The threshold is doing quiet but important work here: the decaying sum is a *fading* trace, but behavior usually wants a crisp yes-or-no. The feedback weight is now a dial for how long the vehicle remembers; we'll work out exactly how to read that dial in a moment.

## Interlude: watch, don't wire

Two fixed circuits worth studying before the capstone. You can't edit these; just pulse them and watch.

### The clamp

Suppose you *want* the indefinite latch from Puzzle 1, since some memories should hold until something clears them, but not the runaway sum. Each tick adds the feedback to whatever the sum already is, and summation nodes don't clamp, so the number genuinely runs away; the motor doesn't care (motors clamp at ±100), but the latch is numerically unstable.

The fix is a **clamping transfer curve** on the feedback wire: any positive value becomes exactly 100, anything at or below zero becomes 0. In the app you draw this by clicking the connection and switching its transfer function to non-linear (see [transfer functions](../guide/transfer-functions)); in the diagram below it's pre-drawn with points at (-100, 0), (0, 0), (1, 100), (100, 100).

One caveat that matters for everything else on this page: a non-linear curve *replaces* the weight, so the clamp ignores whatever number is on the wire. So you can have a clamped hold-forever latch, or a linear decaying one, but the decay has to live in a plain linear weight like your 0.95.

### The tape loop

Here's a tempting wrong idea. Decay is a *fading* memory, strongest right after the tap and trailing off. If what you want is a crisp "hold for exactly two seconds, then release," a long delay looks like the obvious tool: set the delay to **2000 ms** (100 ticks at a 20 ms loop), keep the clamped feedback, and surely the pulse rides the buffer for 2 seconds and falls off the end?

Pulse the bumper below and watch what actually happens.

Tick by tick (a ~200 ms tap is 10 ticks):

| Ticks | What's happening |
|---|---|
| 0–9 (the tap) | Bumper feeds 100; the delay is still reading its *pre-tap zeros*. Sum = 100, and those 100s get written into the buffer. |
| 10–99 | Tap over. The delay is *still* reading zeros; the stored 100s are 2 seconds from re-emerging. Sum = **0**. No hold. |
| 100–109 | The stored 100s finally reach the output. Sum jumps to 100, and the feedback writes them *back into the buffer*. |
| 110–199 | Silence for another ~1.8 s. |
| 200–209 | The echo returns. |
| … | A ~200 ms echo every 2 seconds, forever. |

Instead of a 2-second hold you built a tape loop that never stops playing. On a robot this looks like a twitch into reverse for a fraction of a second every couple of seconds, not the "reverse, then resume" you wanted.

The lesson is worth holding onto: a delay line is a *transport* delay, not a hold. It moves a signal forward in time, unchanged, rather than keeping it present. To keep something present you need feedback that sustains it *right now*, which is exactly what your decaying loop from Puzzle 2 does. For a self-sustaining hold, the decay latch is the right tool, and it comes with a dial: a brief tap charges the sum to a few hundred (a 100 ms tap at feedback 0.95 charges it to about 450), then the charge shrinks by a factor of `w` each tick, crossing the threshold `T` after:

**hold ≈ loopPeriod × ln(T / charge) / ln(w)**

At a 20 ms loop, threshold 10, and a brief tap (charge ≈ 450):

| Feedback weight `w` | Approx. hold |
|---|---|
| 0.90 | 0.7 s |
| 0.95 | 1.5 s |
| 0.98 | 3.8 s |
| 0.99 | 7.6 s |

The weight slider on this page moves in steps of 0.05, which is why every puzzle here uses 0.95. In the app, the weight's numeric box accepts finer values: 0.98 buys you nearly four seconds. (A longer or firmer press charges the sum higher, which stretches the hold a little; the log means it stretches less than you'd guess.) These figures assume this page's deliberate 100 ms tap; a real bumper contact is much briefer and charges the sum to only ~100, so the same weights give shorter holds on hardware, as the [Field Test](../on-the-robot/field-test) page tabulates.

:::tip[Two ways to forget]

Decay and delay embody two different theories of forgetting. **Decay** is a *forgetting curve*: the trace fades smoothly, vivid right after the event and gradually slipping away. This is the mechanism behind the latch you just tuned. **A delay line** is a *reminder*: left open, with no feedback, it holds an event perfectly intact and replays it once, exactly one delay-time later, then lets it go, like a note-to-self that surfaces at a set moment. Wrap that same delay in a feedback loop, though, and the reminder never stops arriving: it reverberates, as you saw above. Nervous systems seem to use both fading traces and precisely-timed reverberation, depending on the topology you wire rather than on the delay alone.
:::

## Puzzle 3: Bump and reverse

The capstone. On the right, a cruise chain: a Forward constant (50) feeds both wheel summations, so the vehicle drives steadily ahead. On the left, your complete decay latch from Puzzle 2, sting and all, charged by the bumper and fading at 0.95 per tick. The two halves don't know about each other yet: the sting's output isn't connected to anything.

Connect memory to behavior. Draw **Still stinging? → Left Sum** and **Still stinging? → Right Sum**, and set both weights to **-1.0**. While the sting is alive it contributes -100 to each sum: 50 + (-100) = -50, and the vehicle backs away. When the trace fades below threshold, the inhibition vanishes and the cruise takes over again, and the vehicle carries on as if nothing happened.

From the outside: the vehicle bonks into a wall, backs up for a second and a half, and resumes driving. A fifty-millisecond contact got amplified into a sustained behavioral response, which is exactly what memory is *for*. Somewhere between Puzzle 1 and here, "output depends only on the present input" stopped being true.

**Going further: a resettable latch.** Sometimes you want to clear a memory on demand, like a "panic button" that cancels a behavior early. Wire a second bump switch into the Latch summation with weight **-1.0**: on the clamped hold-forever latch from the interlude, its -100 exactly cancels the +100 feedback, the sum drops to 0, the delay captures the 0, and the latch is clear one tick later, no matter how much hold time was left. On a decay latch it works too; a held press drains the charge in a few ticks. Try it in the app.

:::note[Weights are limited to ±1]

Connection weights are clamped to the range **-1 to +1**. A single connection can therefore scale a 0–100 signal to at most ±100. To "overpower" a signal more strongly than that, use a [non-linear transfer curve](../guide/transfer-functions), a Threshold, or stack multiple connections, not a weight outside ±1.
:::

## What you've learned

The latch pattern (summation plus delay feedback) converts a transient event into a sustained state, giving a vehicle short-term memory with no storage part anywhere in it. A feedback weight of 1.0 remembers forever (and runs away without a clamp); a weight just below 1.0 turns the loop into a fading trace with a tunable hold time; a threshold turns the fade back into a crisp behavior. A delay alone can never do this job: it transports moments but doesn't hold them.

### Questions to sit with

- The vehicle now does something its sensors cannot: it responds to an event that is no longer happening. Is that enough to say it *remembers*? What more, if anything, would you want before using that word?
- A reactive vehicle is fully explained by its present input. Add a loop and the *same* input can produce different behavior depending on what came before: the vehicle has **state**. Is that the first faint glimmer of an inner life, or just bookkeeping?
- You built "two seconds ago" out of parts that only know "now." Does that make you more impressed by what a brain full of such loops might do, or less?
