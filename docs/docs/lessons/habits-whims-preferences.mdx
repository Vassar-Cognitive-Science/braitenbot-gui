# Habits, Whims & Preferences

Every vehicle you've built so far is an argument between the world and a wire. A sensor reads something out there, a weight scales it, a summation adds it up, and a motor turns. Silence the sensors and the vehicle goes still: there is nothing left in the circuit to say anything. That's been true of every creature in this course, from the light-chaser in [2. Fear & Love](./fear-and-love) to the wall-avoider in [3. Don't](./obstacle-avoidance) to the grudge-holder in [5. Memory](./latches-with-delay). Even the latch, which outlasted its cause by a second and a half, still needed a bump to get started.

This lesson meets the two node types that don't need a cause at all. The **Oscillator** repeats a rhythm on its own schedule, world or no world: a **habit**. The **Noise** node fires unpredictable impulses that answer to nothing: a **whim**. Neither reads a sensor. Neither has an input port. Wire either one straight to a motor and you get motion nobody outside the vehicle asked for.

Alongside them, two nodes you've been using for individual wires all along get promoted to a new job. **Minimum** and **Maximum** don't generate anything themselves, but pointed at a bundle of competing signals they render a verdict: the strongest opinion wins, or a hard ceiling holds no matter how insistent the rest of the circuit gets. That's the closest thing this kit has to a **preference**.

None of this is exotic wiring. Every part below has been on the palette since Lesson 1. What's new is asking what a vehicle looks like once some of its behavior comes from *inside*: a small, bounded personality riding on top of the reflexes you already know how to build.

## A tail with no sensor

Here's an Oscillator wired straight to a **Positional Servo**, with nothing else in the circuit. No sensor, no threshold, no decision of any kind, just a sine wave turning a shaft back and forth.

Watch the readout on `Sway` climb from -100 to 100 and back, twice every four seconds. That's the whole idea of a habit: a signal with a shape, repeating on a clock that belongs to the circuit, not to the room. Feed it to a [Positional Servo](../guide/nodes#positional-servo) and the shape becomes a sweep, ranging from fully one side to fully the other: a tail with no sensor, wagging for its own reasons.

`Sway`'s [Oscillator](../guide/nodes#oscillator) is set to 0.5 Hz (one full swing every two seconds) and its full amplitude of 100. The wire into `Tail` carries all of it. Turn that wire down and the sweep shrinks toward the middle; turn it up and the tail commits fully to each side. Try it yourself.

## A nervous tic

An Oscillator repeats. A **Noise** node doesn't: every tick it emits a fresh random value, bounded by its amplitude but otherwise indifferent to whatever came before. Wire one to a [Digital Output](../guide/nodes#digital-output) and you get a light that flickers on whenever the whim happens to spike past the pin's threshold: a tic with no provocation.

<InteractiveDiagram
  diagram={{

      { id: 'tic', type: 'digital-out', label: 'Tic', x: 60, y: 170, servoPin: '8', threshold: 50 },
    ],
    connections: [
      { id: 'c1', from: 'whim', to: 'tic', weight: 1.0, transferMode: 'linear', transferPoints: [] },
    ],
  }}
  caption="Fidget answers to nothing. Tic only catches the loud moments; most ticks it stays dark."
/>

`Fidget`'s [Noise](../guide/nodes#noise) node draws a fresh value in [-100, 100] every tick; `Tic`'s own threshold (50, the [Digital Output](../guide/nodes#digital-output) default) decides which of those moments are loud enough to light the LED. Turn the connecting wire down and even the loudest whim can no longer reach the threshold: total silence. Below, the wire is turned down too far: `Tic` never fires, no matter how long you watch. Turn the gain back up.

Notice the shape of the two tests: one demands silence, the other demands a spike, and both have to hold on the *same* wiring. That's the honest description of a nervous tic: not "always on" (that's just a light), not "never on" (that's nothing), but bounded and occasional, on a schedule nobody chose.

:::note[Bounded, not caused]
`Fidget` never asks the room a question. There's no photocell, no bumper, nothing upstream of it at all: its number is a property of the node itself, drawn fresh every tick within a fixed range. That's what makes it a *whim* rather than a *reflex*: unpredictable in the moment, but never unbounded. A real nervous tic (an eye twitch, a fidgeting hand) has exactly this character: it isn't triggered by anything you could point to, but it isn't limitless either.
:::

## Which one's closer?

`Sway` and `Fidget` generate signal from nothing. **Maximum** and **Minimum** do the opposite: they generate nothing at all, but they resolve an argument between signals that already exist. Recall the inhibition pattern from [3. Don't](./obstacle-avoidance): a wall on the left throttled the *right* wheel, and a wall on the right throttled the *left* one, each eye wired to its own opposite motor. That works with one eye per side. It breaks the moment you want a *single* "something's close" alarm fed by more than one sensor, because a plain wire can't decide which of two readings to trust.

Here's that vehicle, cruising forward, blind on its right. Its left [ToF Distance](../guide/nodes#tof-distance-vl53l4cd) sensor and a [Maximum](../guide/nodes#maximum) node ("nearer" reads higher, so the *larger* of two distance readings is the *closer* object) are wired up, but the right sensor is still sitting unconnected on the canvas.

One wire, and the arbitration is symmetric: `Nearest` doesn't care *which* eye reported the close call, only that one of them did. `Something Close?` fires, and its -1.0 weight lands on *both* wheel sums at once, so the vehicle brakes evenly rather than pivoting away: it doesn't know which direction is safe anymore, only that it isn't this one. That's a different shape of "no" than Lesson 3's: not a turn, a full stop, exactly the behavior you'd want the instant a decision needs two sensors instead of one.

:::note[Arbitrating real rangefinders]
This is precisely the pattern for combining two physical sensors into one reflex: whatever their number, run every distance-ish reading into a single Maximum, and let the closest threat decide, rather than hand-wiring a separate "no" for every sensor you own. [10. Rangefinders & Bumpers](../on-the-robot/tof-and-bumpers) mounts a real pair of ToF sensors this same arbitration is built for.
:::

## A speed ceiling

Maximum lets the loudest opinion win. **Minimum** does the opposite: wire a signal alongside a Constant and the output can never climb past whatever that constant says, no matter how insistent the signal gets. That's a *ceiling*: a personality trait as much as a safety feature, the vehicle equivalent of "it doesn't matter how excited I get, I don't go past a jog."

Below 60, `Desired Speed` passes straight through and the wheel matches it exactly. [Minimum](../guide/nodes#minimum) of two numbers is just the smaller one, and while `Desired Speed` is smaller, it wins. Push `Desired Speed` past 60 and `Capped Speed` freezes at the ceiling; the motor stops caring how much higher you slide it. No threshold, no clamp curve, no special case: the ceiling is just what "smallest of the inputs" means once one of the inputs stops moving.

## What you've learned

Every earlier lesson built behavior out of the world coming in. This one built behavior out of the vehicle itself: an Oscillator ticking on its own schedule, a Noise node firing whims that answer to nothing, a Maximum resolving two sensors into one verdict, a Minimum holding a hard line no signal can push past. None of these needed a new idea about wiring; every part here has been sitting on the palette since Lesson 1. What changed was noticing that a summation, a threshold, and now a max or a min can face *inward* as easily as outward, and that a vehicle wired that way isn't just reacting anymore.

You've now used every node type this course teaches. The [Nodes reference](../guide/nodes) is the complete catalog, in case you want the exact formula behind any of them one more time.

### Questions to sit with

- `Sway` moves without being asked and `Fidget` fires without being provoked. Is either of those closer to what you'd call a *want*, or are they just numbers with a shape, same as everything else in this course?
- A vehicle with a hard speed ceiling behaves, from the outside, like one that's simply cautious. Is there a difference between "cautious" and "capped," or is caution just what a cap looks like from a distance?
- You arbitrated two sensors into one verdict with a single Maximum node. Real nervous systems do a great deal of this kind of pooling: many noisy inputs collapsed into one decision. Does knowing the mechanism make the resulting behavior feel less like a decision, or does a decision just *mean* this kind of collapsing, all the way down?
